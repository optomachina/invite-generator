01

Compare & pay

6 variants · pick one

The moment you pick the winner and pay $7. This is the revenue moment — it needs to feel decisive and premium, not pushy.

Designer take: Codex keeps making the invite cards huge and drops the iPhone frame; Claude keeps shrinking the invite inside a device mockup. For a product where the invite IS the thing, Codex’s framing wins. Claude’s copy (“Which one feels like Lily?”) is better than anything Codex wrote.
02

Swipe the four

6 variants · pick one

The core mechanic — one card at a time, keep-or-skip, Tinder-style. Should feel like curating, not evaluating.

Designer take: The photographic Codex variant (EMMA on the beach) is the single most Etsy-competitive frame in the audit. It answers “why not Canva?” in one glance. Claude’s photo attempt is just a flat orange rectangle — it missed the brief.
03

The 60-second wait

6 variants · pick one

While the invites render. Must feel like something is being made for you — not like a spinner you’re staring at.

Designer take: Claude’s B nails the voice — “two ready, two still coming in, give it a breath.” It reframes latency as intentional pacing. Codex kept calling it an “AI designer,” which is Canva-tier framing we’re trying to avoid.
04

The share page guests see

6 variants · pick one

When you text the invite link to a friend, this is what they open. Every guest who loves this page is a potential customer — this is marketing, not a file download.

Designer take: The B-balanced Codex illustration (girl on a swing in a garden) is the single most beautiful asset in the whole audit. This is the one guests forward to their group chat. Claude’s share-page attempts use gradient placeholders instead of real illustrations — for a page where the artwork IS the product, that gap is decisive.
05

Telling us about the event

9 variants · pick one

The first real screen. Must feel like texting a friend about your kid’s party — not filling out a form.

Designer take: Claude went deep on voice-first intake and invented four variants. A4 (“three small questions” pull-quote with an answered line struck through) looks like a designer’s notebook. A2 (live transcript while you talk) is the most production-ready voice UI I’ve seen in this category. Codex retreated to centered-hero + category chips — safer but more Typeform-y.
